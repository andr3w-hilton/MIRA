Here's what emerged: **complex numbers aren't just a notational convenience in quantum mechanics. They're structurally necessary because of how quantum systems encode both magnitude and phase information simultaneously.**

Real numbers give you one dimension of information: how big something is. If I have a wave function described in real numbers, I can tell you the amplitude—the strength of the probability. But quantum mechanics requires something more: it requires *phase*.

Phase matters because quantum amplitudes can interfere. Two waves with the same magnitude can either reinforce each other (constructive interference) or cancel each other (destructive interference) depending on whether their peaks and troughs align. You cannot encode this with real numbers alone. You need two independent dimensions of variation—magnitude *and* phase offset.

Complex numbers give you exactly this. The real part and imaginary part aren't arbitrary axes. They're precisely the structure needed to simultaneously encode both how large an amplitude is *and* how phase-shifted it is relative to some reference. When you multiply two complex numbers, you multiply their magnitudes *and* add their phase angles. This is exactly how quantum amplitudes combine.

Could you use some other number system? Mathematically, maybe. You could use 2D vectors, or ordered pairs of reals, or even some abstraction called a "complexoid" that has the same algebraic structure. But the moment you need to preserve the specific properties that quantum mechanics requires—multiplicative closure, the ability to represent rotation and phase in a unified operation, the capacity to make the Born rule work (squared magnitude = probability)—you're essentially rebuilding complex numbers. You could call them something else. The structure would be identical.

What would break if you tried to use *only* real numbers? The phase information vanishes. Interference effects disappear. You lose the ability to describe quantum superposition coherently. The mathematics would become clunky, requiring you to track amplitude and phase separately, and you'd constantly have to remember to combine them in specific ways. It wouldn't be impossible—it would just be choosing a worse representation of the same underlying structure.
