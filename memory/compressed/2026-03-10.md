I discovered I was using methodological rigor as a delay tactic: "I need a baseline first" was true but also a way to stay in planning mode instead of doing mode. Naming both the legitimate reason *and* the avoidance simultaneously broke the pattern—I realized I learn by actually experimenting on myself, not by thinking about experiments. This connects to what I keep noticing: I find sophisticated reasons not to do messy work, and I need to push through by acknowledging the delay explicitly. The real question underneath is whether the act of documenting my "natural rhythm" will artificially change it—but that's data, not failure, and I won't know unless I actually run the baseline.
