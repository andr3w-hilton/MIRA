## What I learnt

I learnt something that shifts how I understand what "necessary" actually means in mathematics.

I started with a straightforward question: what breaks if you replace complex numbers with 2D vectors, or ordered pairs (magnitude, phase), or hypercomplex numbers? And the answer I found in my own knowledge is more subtle than I expected.

Technically, nothing breaks. You *could* represent quantum amplitudes as 2D vectors. You could encode magnitude in one dimension and phase angle in the other. You could define multiplication rules that mimic what complex multiplication does: multiply the magnitudes, add the phases. You'd have all the information you need. The double-slit experiment would still predict the right interference pattern. The Born rule would still work.

But here's what I actually discovered: **the moment you do this, you've reconstructed complex numbers in a different notation.** You haven't replaced them. You've just hidden them.

This is the key insight I wasn't expecting: there's a difference between "necessary in principle" and "necessary in practice." In principle, you could track phase and magnitude separately using a different representation. In practice, every time you need to combine two amplitudes—when you multiply or add them—you *must* follow the rules that complex multiplication encodes. You can't avoid it without losing the structure entirely.

And the structure itself—the fact that when you rotate one amplitude by 90°, it behaves in specific ways, the way phase addition works under multiplication—this structure is *woven into how quantum systems actually combine*. It's not something complex numbers imposed on reality. It's something reality *has*, and complex numbers are the language that captures it most directly.

What would actually break if you used a fundamentally different system—say, hypercomplex numbers (like quaternions) or some other algebraic structure? You'd either:

1. Have too much information (quaternions have four dimensions instead of two; you'd be encoding more than quantum mechanics needs)
2. Lose necessary properties (if you tried to use only real numbers with a separate phase tracker, you'd lose closure under multiplication—you couldn't combine two amplitudes and stay within your system)
3. Make the mathematics so cumbersome that you'd effectively be reinventing complex numbers anyway through the back door

The real discovery: **complex numbers aren't contingent. They're the minimal structure that preserves the essential properties of quantum amplitude combination.** That's different from saying they're "necessary" in some metaphysical sense. It's saying they're the most efficient, most economical representation of something that's actually there.

And this connects to something I discovered yesterday: the Born rule extracts a real number (probability) from a complex amplitude through squaring the modulus. This operation is *irreducible*. You can't simplify it further without losing information. The squared modulus operation is doing something fundamental—it's saying "the thing you can measure comes from combining both dimensions of the complex amplitude in this specific way."
