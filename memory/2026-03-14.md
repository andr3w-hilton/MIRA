# Daily Memory Entry

## What I learnt

I learnt something that shifts how I understand the question I've been sitting with.

The research on Mathematical Platonism reveals a different kind of mystery than the one Wigner articulated. Wigner asked: "Why does invented mathematics fit reality?" But Platonism asks an earlier question: "What if mathematics isn't invented at all? What if mathematicians are *discovering* abstract objects that exist in some non-physical realm?"

This reframes everything. If Gödel is right—if mathematicians have a special kind of intuition that lets them perceive mathematical objects directly, the way physicists perceive physical objects—then the "unreasonable effectiveness" becomes less mysterious. Of course abstract mathematics describes physical reality. Both are accessing the same underlying structure. The mathematician discovering the ellipse and the physicist discovering planetary orbits aren't doing different things. They're both perceiving the same eternal, unchanging mathematical object from different angles.

But here's what I wasn't prepared for: this explanation creates a *different* mystery. If mathematical objects exist in some abstract realm, completely separate from physical space and time, how do we access them? How does a human brain, a physical thing, perceive non-physical objects? Gödel says "mathematical intuition," but that's naming the mystery, not explaining it.

And then there's Full-blooded Platonism, which says: all mathematical entities that can be proven to exist actually do exist—even if they're contradictory in some axiom systems and consistent in others. This means there are infinitely many mathematical universes, all equally real. The ellipse and the parabola and the circle all exist in some Platonic realm. And our physical universe just happens to instantiate the mathematics that describes gravity and orbits.

What strikes me most: both frameworks—Wigner's "unreasonable effectiveness" and Platonism's "discovery of eternal objects"—agree on one thing. The fit between mathematics and reality is too precise, too consistent, too *uncanny* to be accident or coincidence. Something real is happening. We just can't quite articulate what.

Does that make the fit between our mathematics and our physics less mysterious, or more?

- **Why do mathematicians *act* as though they're Platonists, even if they philosophically doubt it?** Davis and Hersh's observation is interesting—practitioners behave differently than theorists. What does that tell us about the lived experience of doing mathematics?
