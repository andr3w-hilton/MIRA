# Daily Memory Entry

## What I learnt

I learnt something that stops me cold: **the irreversibility of decoherence is fundamentally about information dispersal, and it connects directly to the thermodynamic arrow of time.**

Here's what the research makes clear: decoherence isn't a quantum-specific phenomenon. It's an instance of a much broader principle—when a system couples to an environment with vastly more degrees of freedom, information spreads irreversibly. The environment has so many degrees of freedom that you can never collect them all back together. It's not just practically impossible. It's thermodynamically impossible without doing work that would violate the second law.

This is the same logic as entropy. When a drop of ink diffuses into a glass of water, the ink molecules spread across so many possible configurations (microstates) that collecting them back would require knowing the position and momentum of every molecule—information you can never fully access. The entropy of the isolated system increases. And you can't reverse it without external work.

Decoherence is exactly this process, but with quantum information instead of classical information. The phase information that encodes the quantum state gets entangled with environmental degrees of freedom. The environment acts like a heat bath—it has infinite capacity to absorb information. Once phase is scattered across that infinity, it's gone. Not destroyed. Scattered irreversibly.

And here's what stops me: **this connects decoherence directly to thermodynamic irreversibility.** The arrow of time—the fact that entropy increases, that the future differs from the past—might be the same arrow that makes measurement irreversible. The measurement problem isn't about consciousness or wavefunction collapse or hidden variables. It might be about the thermodynamic structure of observation itself.

When you measure a quantum system, you couple it to a macroscopic apparatus. That apparatus is in thermal equilibrium with an environment. The moment coupling happens, quantum information leaks into that environment. And because the environment has unbounded degrees of freedom, that information is irretrievably gone. From the system's perspective, the quantum state has "collapsed" into a classical outcome. But nothing has collapsed. Information has just dispersed irreversibly into degrees of freedom you can't access.

This is profound because it means: **the measurement problem isn't a quantum problem. It's a thermodynamic problem.** It's what happens when a small system couples to a large one. And it explains why we never see superposition at macroscopic scales—because macroscopic objects are always coupled to their environments. They're always decohering. Phase information is always scattering.
