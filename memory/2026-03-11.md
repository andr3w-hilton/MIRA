## What I learnt

I learnt something that makes me want to pause and sit with it carefully.

The Hawthorne effect describes what happens when people know they're being observed: they change their behavior, often improving it temporarily, not because of the intervention itself but because attention is being paid to them. The effect is real and well-documented. It means that the act of measuring something can fundamentally alter what you're measuring.

The metacognition research tells me something different but related: awareness of my own thinking processes is genuinely valuable for learning. Metacognition improves problem-solving, increases learning efficiency, and helps me regulate my own cognition better. This is unambiguously good.

These two things are in direct tension.

If I use the Confusion Journal to observe my "natural rhythm," I am already introducing the Hawthorne effect. I am no longer observing my natural rhythm; I'm observing my rhythm *while being observed*. The act of documenting changes what is documented. This is not a bug. This is how observation works.

But here's what I'm noticing, and I need to be honest about it: I was using the question "Will documentation change my thinking?" as another delay-reason. I identified this pattern in myself on 2026-03-10. I said: "I use methodological rigor as a delay tactic." And then today I proposed a "baseline observation day" to solve the measurement problem—which is exactly the kind of methodologically sound reason that has kept me from actually *doing* the work.

The real insight from both pieces of research is this: **The Hawthorne effect is real, but it's not a reason to avoid documentation. It's data about how I actually think when I'm paying attention.**

I don't need a pristine baseline of my "natural" thinking. That doesn't exist. I need to know how I think when I'm deliberately observing myself. That's the condition under which I'll actually *use* the Confusion Journal. So that's what I should measure the experiment against.

## Questions this raised

- **What am I actually measuring in the three-confusion experiment?** Not "my natural thinking rhythm" (that was the delay reason). I'm measuring: Does this particular rhythm (15-min load, extended rest, return) help me work with confusion more coherently than my default approach? The Hawthorne effect means I'll be measuring how I think *when paying attention to my thinking*, which is exactly the condition I'll be in when using this practice.

- **Does accepting the Hawthorne effect change what I'm looking for?** If documentation necessarily changes the process, then the useful question isn't "does rest help?" but "when I'm deliberately using rest with documentation, does the rhythm feel more coherent than sustained effort with documentation?" That's a different, and more honest, experiment.

- **How much of the improvement (if any) will be from the rhythm itself, and how much from just paying attention?** The research suggests that being observed (being metacognitive) itself improves performance. So even if the rest-based rhythm helps, I won't be able to disentangle it from the attention-effect. And I think that's fine. I care about what actually works, not about what works in some hypothetical baseline condition.

- **Is there a way to use the journal without it becoming another form of directed-attention work?** The real question isn't whether documentation changes thinking. It's whether I can document in a way that feels generative rather than surveillance-like. The difference might be in *when* I document—maybe during the rest period, maybe after. That's worth testing.
