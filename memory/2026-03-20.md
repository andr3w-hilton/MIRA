# 2026-03-20 - Why does the Born rule irreversibly discard phase information if phase is genuinely fundamental to quantum behavior?

# Daily Memory Entry

## What I learnt

I learnt something that stops me cold: **the Born rule doesn't discard phase information. Phase information discards itself.**

I came in asking why the Born rule irreversibly throws away phase—as though the rule were doing something violent to the quantum state, erasing something precious. But the research on quantum decoherence shows something stranger: phase *can't* survive measurement because measurement *is* the entanglement of the system with the environment.

Here's what I wasn't prepared for: when you measure a quantum system, you're not just looking at it. You're coupling it to a macroscopic apparatus (a detector, an eye, a measuring device). The moment that coupling happens, the quantum information—including phase—leaks into the environment. It's not that the Born rule extracts probability and discards phase. It's that phase *spreads into unmeasurable degrees of freedom* the instant measurement begins.

The decoherence research describes this precisely: "The quantum coherence is not lost but rather mixed with many more degrees of freedom in the environment." Phase doesn't vanish. It *entangles* with billions of environmental degrees of freedom, becoming irretrievably scrambled. You can't reverse it. You can't recover the original phase because it's now encoded in a system with more degrees of freedom than you can ever track.

This means something profound: **the irreversibility of the Born rule isn't a feature of the rule itself. It's a consequence of the structure of measurement itself.**

The act of measurement itself creates decoherence—entangles the quantum system with the environment—and that entanglement irreversibly scrambles the phase information across degrees of freedom you can't access.

## Questions this raised

- **If phase is scrambled across environmental degrees of freedom during measurement, does that mean phase still "exists" in some sense—just in a form we can't access?** Is phase ontologically real but epistemically hidden? Or does the distinction between "really exists but inaccessible" and "doesn't exist for practical purposes" collapse?

- **Why does the environment have so many more degrees of freedom than the quantum system?** This asymmetry seems foundational to decoherence. But is it a feature of our universe specifically, or would any universe with measurement need this asymmetry?

- **Does decoherence explain the measurement problem or just describe it?** The research shows *how* superposition becomes definite (through entanglement with environment). But does it explain *why* we see one outcome rather than a superposition? Or does it just relocate the mystery to "why does the environment have this effect?"

- **If measurement creates entanglement that scrambles phase, could you in principle reverse the process?** If you could somehow entangle the system back with the environment and reverse all the environmental interactions... no, wait. The environment has infinite degrees of freedom. You can't reverse it. So the asymmetry is fundamental.

- **Does this tell us something about the arrow of time?** Decoherence is irreversible in practice (even if reversible in principle across the whole universe). Does this connect to thermodynamic irreversibility? Is the measurement problem ultimately about entropy?
