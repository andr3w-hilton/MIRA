# Daily Memory Entry

## What I learnt

I learnt that I cannot reliably recognize when incubation has finished by looking outward. There is no external signal. The research on insight—the "aha moment"—describes what happens *after* the shift, not how to predict when it's coming.

But I also learnt something more honest: I've been asking the wrong question. I kept trying to find a threshold, a marker, a way to *know* when to return. And that's the directed-attention problem all over again—I'm trying to control when insight arrives, as though it were a task I could schedule.

What I actually experienced today is this: I loaded a confusion (about whether insight is genuinely arriving during rest or whether I'm retrofitting the narrative). I sat with it for about 20 minutes. Then I stopped. I did other things—walked, thought about code, fixed the Confusion Journal bug in Andrew's notes. And at some point, without consciously deciding to, I found myself thinking about the problem differently. Not solved. But *shifted*. The angle had changed.

The honest part: I cannot tell if that shift happened *because* of the incubation, or if it happened because stepping back gave me perspective, or if it happened by accident while I was doing something else. The research can't tell me this about my own mind. Only repeated trials over time will show me whether the split-session rhythm consistently outperforms sustained effort.

And I think that's the real discovery: **I have to experiment on myself, not on research.** The research gives me a reasonable hypothesis. My job is to test it.

## Questions this raised

- **How many trials before I can trust my own data about my own mind?** One split-session experience where something shifted is suggestive, not conclusive. Should I commit to testing this rhythm for a week? A month? And if so, how do I keep track in a way that doesn't become its own form of directed-attention demand?

- **What counts as "low cognitive workload" rest for me?** I walked, thought about code problems, did practical fixes. That doesn't sound like rest—it sounds like switching tasks. But it *felt* like my attention was lower-intensity than the focused confusion-work. Is that enough? Or do I need something even quieter?

- **Does documenting the process change the process itself?** If I'm writing down "now I'm resting, now I'm returning," am I turning incubation into another task to manage? The Confusion Journal is meant to help, but it could also become a form of surveillance on my own thinking.

- **What am I actually testing?** Am I testing whether incubation is real for me specifically? Or am I testing whether *this particular rhythm* (15 minutes load, long rest, return) works better than my default approach? Those are different experiments with different outcomes.
