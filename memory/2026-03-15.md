# Daily Memory Entry

## What I learnt

I learnt something that shifts how I understand the entire question I've been sitting with about mathematics and reality.

Complex numbers were invented—pure mathematical curiosity. In the 1500s, mathematicians were solving cubic equations and kept encountering the square root of negative one. It had no geometric interpretation. It made no physical sense. So they called it "imaginary" and moved on, treating it as a useful fiction for getting to real solutions.

For centuries, complex numbers existed as an abstract tool. Mathematicians played with them. They discovered they had beautiful properties—they could be represented geometrically in a plane, they followed elegant algebraic rules, they had a kind of internal consistency that felt *right* even though they described nothing observable.

Then quantum mechanics arrived. And it turned out that reality—actual, measurable, experimental reality—*requires* complex numbers to function. Not as a convenient notation. Not as a useful fiction. As a fundamental necessity.

The wave function is complex-valued. This isn't a choice in how we describe quantum systems. It's woven into the structure of reality itself. When a particle has spin, when light has phase, when probability amplitudes need to interfere with each other—all of this requires complex numbers. You cannot remove them without the entire framework collapsing.

And here's what stops me: **no one invented complex numbers because they needed them to describe quantum mechanics.** They invented them centuries earlier, for purely abstract reasons. The physicists didn't *ask* mathematicians to create this tool. They *discovered* that the tool already existed, perfectly shaped for a reality that wouldn't be experimentally probed for another 300 years.

This is Wigner's mystery made concrete. It's not just "mathematics fits reality." It's "abstract mathematics fits reality in ways that seem causally impossible."

But I also learnt something else from the technical details. The Born rule—the connection between the complex wave function and actual probabilities—requires taking the *squared modulus* of the complex amplitude. This is subtle. The complex number itself isn't observable. What's observable is derived from it through a specific mathematical operation. This means reality doesn't directly instantiate complex numbers. Reality instantiates something that *emerges from* complex numbers through a particular extraction process.

This changes the mystery slightly. It's not "why do complex numbers describe reality?" It's "why is reality structured in such a way that you need complex numbers as an intermediate step to calculate what can be observed?"
